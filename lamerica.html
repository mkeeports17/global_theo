Christianity in Latin America

  • An estimate of 8,000 to 10,000 people convert daily to evangelicalism, most of them coming from the Roman Catholic Church.
  • 20% of Latin Americans were reported to be evangelical Christian in 2020.
  • The evangelical church in Brazil grew from 5.3% of the population in 1970, to 15.5% in 2000, 22.2% in 2010, 22.2% in 2010, and up to 31% of the Brazilian population by 2022.
  • The Roman Catholic church in Brazil declined from 92% members in 1970, 73.6% in 2000, 64.6% in 2010, and it now represents ~50% of the nation’s population.

These shocking statistics show why researchers predict that by 2032 the Evangelical church will be larger than the Roman Catholic church. There was a survey by Pew Research that showed the most frequent reason for leaving the Catholic church for Protestantism was because they were seeking a more personal connection with God. Many former Catholics also said they became Protestants because they wanted a different style of worship and a church that helps its members more. 58% of them said that their new church reached out to them.

Christians in Latin America align closely with the Liberation Theology method of analyzing theological concepts. This is the way of doing theology that begins with praxis within the contexts of poverty and oppression. It includes a holistic approach seeking to understand the people’s history of suffering, which it’s the preferential option for the poor. The underlying framework behind this theology is seeking to understand why God consistently takes the side of the poor, oppressed, and marginalized. They look at what this says about God and how he wants us to relate to others. Christians in Latin America also relate to the indigenizing principle explained on the Africa page. There has been a long and privileged presence of Christianity, consisting of all different variations of the religion which have all been mixed together and ‘simmering’ for centuries. This includes influences from all kinds of different cultures, which has shaped the culture of Christians in Latin America. Christians in the area seek to connect the lessons they’ve learned from experiences to their lives. They also seek to make Christianity more relevant to their contexts. An example of indigenized christology in Latin America is the celebration of the Day of the Dead. It is a celebration that has been a tradition for Latin American countries since their ancestors. The idea is that they celebrate their loved ones because they know their spirits are alive and breathing in a better place. This relates to the indigenizing principle because they used the biblical idea of raising from the dead body and into a better place, referred to as heaven by christians. Although their traditions celebrate things a little differently than northern christians, they still infused ideas of God's redemption into their traditions and continue to praise God.
