Africa

Christianity in Africa

African evangelists are known for their charismatic and exhilarating praise, consisting of loud, upbeat praise songs and clapping and dancing. Through worship they celebrate God's acts of salvation of spirit, soul, and body. Christians in Africa typically use Cultural Theology to view Jesus and the scriptures. This is a way of doing theology which seeks to understand christian faith from the perspectives of specific identities (ethnicity, gender, etc..). It is aligned with the indigenizing principle, which emphasizes that all Christians have the same access to gospel no matter their past or culture. It also tells Christians they should be comfortable in their own church, meaning it’s ok to embrace God in your own culture. The cultural theology also focuses on Jesus as the Chief, which is a term for God more applicable to African culture. Jesus is like a chief because he is “strong”, generous and wise, the reconciler, and the mediator.

The African traditional worldview sees Satan and his demonic forces as the cause of life's problems. They believe that you must neutralize the attacks of Satan through spiritual war. Their prayers typically ask for God's hand in deliverance from curses and evil, as well as healing from diseases and financial wealth. Sometimes the church practices spiritual fasting together as a whole. African Christians also view the Bible as God's Word to guide humanity in matters of faith and practice. They also interpret the Bible literally and use their native tongues to translate the Word in their own language, gaining extra insight that may be erased when translated to different languages such as English. Another interesting thing about the Christian African society is that the group’s interest is more important than the individual’s. They go the extra mile to make sure the needs of the group are met, instead of just tending to their own needs. This is a way for them to share the selfless love of Jesus. The church community of Christian Africans also typically shares times of celebration, such as marriages or childbirths, as well as sharing times of sorrow, such as death and sickness. Last but not least, another part of the church life is a passion for evangelism. They often throw together social welfare and social justice projects. A large cause for the growth of Christianity in Africa is the evangelical activities hosted by the church community such as personal witnessing, door to door evangelism, concerts, and film/media.
